Credit Score Basics in United States: What Actually Moves the Number

The most widely used credit score in the U.S. is the FICO Score, ranging from 300–850. VantageScore is a common alternative with a similar range. Lenders use these scores to gauge how risky it is to lend you money.

The five FICO factors

Payment history (about 35%) — Whether you've paid past credit accounts on time. This is the single biggest factor.

Amounts owed / credit utilization (about 30%) — How much of your available credit you're using. Keeping utilization under roughly 30%, ideally under 10%, tends to help scores the most.

Length of credit history (about 15%) — The age of your oldest account, newest account, and average age of all accounts.

Credit mix (about 10%) — Having a mix of account types (cards, installment loans) can help slightly.

New credit (about 10%) — Recent applications and hard inquiries.

Score ranges, roughly

300–579 poor, 580–669 fair, 670–739 good, 740–799 very good, 800–850 exceptional. Each issuer sets its own approval threshold.

Common myths

Checking your own score doesn't hurt it. You don't need to carry a balance to build credit — paying in full every month builds credit just as well.
