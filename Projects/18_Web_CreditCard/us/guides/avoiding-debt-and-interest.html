Avoiding Credit Card Debt and Interest Charges

Used well, a credit card is a payment tool with a grace period and rewards attached. Used poorly, it's one of the most expensive ways to borrow, since card interest rates are typically far higher than other consumer loans. A few habits make the difference.

Pay the statement balance in full

The single most effective habit is paying your full statement balance by the due date every cycle. This preserves your grace period, meaning purchases don't accrue interest at all.

Treat it like a debit card

A simple mental model: only charge what you could pay off today if you had to.

Set up autopay for at least the minimum

Missing a payment entirely can trigger late fees, a higher penalty rate, and damage to your payment history. Autopay for at least the minimum is a safety net.

Watch cash withdrawals

Cash advances/withdrawals on a credit card typically have no grace period, a higher interest rate, and an upfront fee. Avoid these except in real emergencies.

Use 0%/low-interest offers deliberately

Balance transfer and low-interest plans (see our low-interest roundup) can legitimately help pay down debt, but only with a concrete plan to clear the balance before the promotional period ends.

If you're already carrying a balance

Prioritize paying more than the minimum, focus extra payments on the highest-rate balance first, and use our balance transfer savings calculator to check whether moving the balance would actually save money once fees are accounted for.
