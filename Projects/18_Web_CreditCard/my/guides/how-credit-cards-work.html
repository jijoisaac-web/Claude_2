How Credit Cards Actually Work

A credit card is a revolving line of credit issued by a bank. Instead of paying with money you already have, you're borrowing from the issuer up to a set credit limit, and agreeing to pay it back — either in full or over time.

The billing cycle

Every card has a billing cycle, typically around 30 days. At the end of the cycle, the issuer generates a statement showing everything you charged, any payments or credits, and the total balance owed, followed by a due date roughly 18–25 days later.

The grace period

If you pay your statement balance in full by the due date, most cards charge no interest on purchases — the grace period. It only applies if you started from a zero balance and pay in full every cycle.

Minimum payments vs. full payments

Issuers only require a small minimum payment each cycle, often around 5% of the balance in many markets. Paying only the minimum keeps the account in good standing, but interest accrues on the remaining balance — which is why carrying a balance on a card gets expensive quickly.

Interest rates

Card interest is usually quoted as a monthly or annual rate and applied to your average daily balance. Rates vary by issuer, card, and your credit profile, so always check the specific card's terms rather than assuming a rate.

Fees to know

Beyond interest, common fees include annual fees (charged once a year to hold the card), late payment fees, foreign currency transaction fees, cash withdrawal fees, and balance transfer processing fees.
